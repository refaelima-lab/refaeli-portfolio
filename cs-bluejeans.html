01 · Context

Building the digital classroom
as the world moved online.

As Design Lead for the Remote Learning product, I helped shape a virtual classroom experience designed to support both teaching and human connection at scale.

The platform was adopted by organizations including Verizon Training, the University of Michigan, Wharton School of Pennsylvania, and many others — validating its impact across education and enterprise learning environments.

The challenge wasn't building a video tool. It was designing a system capable of holding the complexity of a real classroom: teaching, visibility, communication, engagement, and management — simultaneously.

Digital communication became essential infrastructure during the pandemic. The question wasn't whether to build — it was how to build something human enough to hold real learning.

05 · User Research & Testing

Research that adapted
to pandemic constraints.

During the pandemic, traditional in-person research became impossible. User testing shifted entirely online through platforms including usabilityhub and usertesting.com.

Through extensive A/B testing, heat map analysis, event tracking, and expert interviews, we continuously refined the product around real classroom behavior rather than assumptions.

Educators across multiple grade levels participated in cognitive walkthroughs, helping validate whether the platform addressed the realities of teaching, engagement, and classroom management in a remote environment.

Remote user testing sessions — pandemic-adapted research methodology
06 · Key Insights

What the research
actually revealed.

The insights were behavioral, not cosmetic. They changed what we built — not just how we built it.

Cognitive Load

Instructors managing a live classroom were operating at cognitive capacity. Every extra click was an error waiting to happen. Controls needed to be anticipatory, not reactive.

Visibility Gap

Students felt invisible in large classes. When they couldn't see themselves or gauge participation, engagement dropped. Presence signals mattered more than we expected.

Context Switching

Educators lost significant time moving between tools — slides, roster, chat, participant controls. Reducing context switching was the highest-leverage design intervention.

Trust in the Platform

Technical failures during live sessions caused disproportionate loss of student trust. Stability and recovery behavior mattered more than any feature.

Onboarding Friction

First-session setup consistently blocked instructors from focusing on teaching. The FTU experience needed to establish confidence, not just configure settings.

Grade-Level Variance

Classroom needs varied sharply between K-12, higher ed, and enterprise. A one-size UI failed all of them. Configurability wasn't optional — it was the product.

12 · Preferences Redesign

Restructuring the most
heavily used area.

The Preferences redesign restructured one of the most heavily used areas of the product, improving discoverability, reducing friction, and modernizing the experience across the desktop ecosystem.

The existing preferences panel had grown organically — a reflection of years of feature additions without architectural reconsideration. Users couldn't find settings they used regularly. Power users had built workarounds. New users were blocked entirely.

The redesign started with card sorting research, reclassified every setting by user mental model rather than engineering category, and introduced progressive disclosure to reduce the visible surface area without reducing capability.

13 · Name Tag

Presence in a
virtual room.

Identity and presence in virtual meetings. Name tags made participants visible and contextual — a small design decision with a significant impact on meeting dynamics.

14 · Smart Theme (POC)

Early AI.
Contextual personalization.

Smart Theme was a proof-of-concept exploring intelligent environment adaptation — the meeting interface automatically adjusting its visual configuration based on context, time of day, and participant signals.

This was early AI experimentation: not a chatbot, not a feature-flagged option, but a system-level behavior that made the product feel contextually aware without demanding user input.

The POC demonstrated that AI in productivity tools works best when it removes decisions rather than adding them.

15 · Schedule Meetings In-App

Ecosystem simplification.
Reducing context switching.

Scheduling a meeting shouldn't require leaving the meeting tool. The in-app scheduling feature unified the calendar workflow with the video collaboration experience — reducing the tool-switching that fragmented the meeting preparation flow.

The design challenge was integrating calendar data, participant management, and meeting settings into a single flow without overwhelming the interface with a separate application.

17 · Atomic Design System

900+ components.

I led the creation of a comprehensive Atomic Design System in Figma, consisting of more than 900 responsive components designed to scale across multiple products and platforms.

The system unified branding, accelerated collaboration between design and engineering, and dramatically improved the speed of product iteration and delivery.

The system architecture followed strict atomic principles — tokens, atoms, molecules, organisms — ensuring that every component could compose predictably regardless of context, platform, or team.
