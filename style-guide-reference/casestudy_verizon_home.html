01 · Context

I didn't inherit a clean slate.
I inherited a mess.

When I joined the Verizon Home App, it was everything customers dreaded about utility apps: opaque errors, fragmented flows, and a support queue generating 1,600+ "app unusable" tickets per release cycle. Nobody trusted it.

Leadership had already tried patching individual screens. What they hadn't done was ask the harder question: why did this product exist, and for whom?

My first three weeks weren't spent in Figma. They were spent listening — to customer data, to support transcripts, to engineers who had been silently compensating for design debt for years. What I found wasn't a UX problem. It was a strategy problem wearing a UX costume.

The app had been built to reduce call volume — a cost-center metric. Not to serve customers. That misalignment was embedded in every decision: the information hierarchy, the error states, the onboarding. It optimized for deflection, not trust.

The real problem wasn't the interface. It was that nobody had defined what "success" looked like from the customer's perspective — and I had to make that case before I could design a single screen.

02 · The Problem

The wrong thing,
beautifully executed

"Most products fail because they solve the wrong thing beautifully. I design systems that resolve what matters, not interfaces that mask it."

Design Philosophy · Refaeli Ma

Through research synthesis, competitive analysis, and direct customer interviews, three failure patterns emerged. None of them were about color, typography, or layout.

Discovery

Reactive, not proactive. The app only surfaced when something was wrong. No ambient presence, no guidance during the calm stretches when customers were most receptive.

Trust Gap

Error states broke trust completely. When the app failed, it gave customers nothing — no context, no next step. Silence reads as incompetence. Support queues spiked every release.

System Fragmentation

Eleven teams, no shared language. Inconsistent components meant inconsistent experiences. Every team was solving the same problems in isolation, compounding debt with every sprint.

03 · Strategy

You can't design your way
out of a strategy problem

My first move wasn't a redesign. It was a reframe. I brought product, engineering, and business stakeholders into a room and asked a simple question: what do we want customers to feel three months after activating service? That question reoriented the entire roadmap.

01
Reposition the product's identity

Shifted the product mental model from "troubleshooting tool" to "proactive AI advisor." This wasn't a brand exercise — it restructured priorities across PM, engineering, and content strategy.

02
Fix trust before adding features

Redesigned error states and onboarding before building anything new. Trust is cumulative. One bad moment erases ten good ones. We had to stop the bleeding before we could grow.

03
Build a shared design language

Created scalable cross-platform standards that 11 teams could actually use. Reduced design-to-dev misalignment and gave the product a coherent identity across 10+ release cycles.

04
Embed AI as intelligence, not novelty

Defined the AI strategy around personalized guidance and smart task automation — not chatbots. The criterion: does this make the right action feel inevitable? If not, it didn't ship.

04 · Leadership

The work behind
the work

Principal-level design isn't about being the best individual contributor in the room. It's about making the whole room better — changing how decisions get made, not just what the decisions are.

Here's how I approached it at Verizon.

Months 1–2
Listen before designing

Embedded in support, sat with engineers, audited 18 months of release notes. Built a shared picture of the problem before proposing anything. This built credibility that lasted years.

Months 3–5
Align across leadership

Mapped the product's stakeholder landscape. Identified where decisions were being made that shouldn't be, and surfaced design earlier in the process — before scope was locked.

Months 6–12
Build the infrastructure, not the feature

Prioritized the design system and cross-functional operating model over individual screens. The first 900-component system felt slow. By release 4, every team was moving faster.

Ongoing
Grow designers, not dependents

Mentored by asking better questions, not giving answers. Established critique rituals that built craft standards without creating gatekeeping. The goal was a team that didn't need me to decide.

06 · Reflection

What I'd tell
my earlier self

The most important design work I did on this project happened before any screens existed. Defining what problem we were actually solving, who would own that definition, and how we'd know if we'd succeeded — that's the work that made everything else possible.

I also learned that the most important resource I had wasn't my craft. It was trust. Trust from PMs to challenge scope. Trust from engineers to push back on timelines. Trust from designers to tell me when I was wrong.

That trust was built slowly, through consistency, through being right sometimes and wrong gracefully the other times, and through never making it about me.

  • Reframing the problem is the design work. The prettiest solution to the wrong problem is a beautiful failure.
  • Infrastructure before features, always. A good design system multiplies your team. Skipping it multiplies your debt.
  • Trust is the only deliverable that compounds. Every credibility withdrawal takes ten deposits to rebuild.
  • Good design is invisible. If customers notice the interface, something went wrong. Success looks like nothing at all.
  • Lead by raising the ceiling, not lowering the floor. Mentor toward independence, not toward need.
