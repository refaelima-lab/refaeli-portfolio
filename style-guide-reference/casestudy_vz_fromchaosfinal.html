01 · Context

MVP to crisis.
Crisis to system change.

The Verizon Home App launched as an MVP — a half-built product pushed to market before it was ready. By version 1.9, the iOS rating had dropped to 1.7. The app was functionally broken for a significant portion of customers, and nobody had a clear owner for fixing it.

I joined mid-crisis, transitioning from BlueJeans by Verizon. What I found wasn't a design problem. It was an organizational problem: 10 teams, 10 roadmaps, agency handoffs with missing PRDs, late feasibility checks, and a feedback loop that was generating noise instead of signal.

Nobody was working on the wrong things intentionally. They were just working without a shared picture of what "right" looked like.

I design for the problem beneath the problem. Most products fail because they solve the wrong thing beautifully. I design systems that resolve what matters, not interfaces that mask it. Good design is invisible. It shapes behavior, until the right thing feels inevitable.

02 · Problem Blocking Value

Three failures.
None of them were about pixels.

The surface problems were obvious — bad ratings, broken flows, frustrated customers. The underlying problems were organizational. Until those were named, no design work would stick.

🔀
Fragmented Delivery
No process, no ownership
  • Agency handoffs with missing PRDs
  • No intake → feasibility → design → QA flow
  • Late feasibility checks causing constant churn
  • Teams reacting instead of planning
🎯
Misaligned Success
DAU/MAU ≠ real success
  • KPI drift across 10 separate roadmaps
  • Tech-driven product decisions, not user-driven
  • Endless meetings, no shared prioritization model
  • No agreed definition of what "better" looked like
🔇
Missing Feedback Loop
Feedback without signal
  • Customer pain existed, but it was noise — unstructured
  • Blind spots at every touchpoint
  • Teams working on assumptions, not insights
  • Solutions shipped before problems were defined
03 · Finding My Position

Not just filling gaps.
Bridging them.

Principal-level design means operating across three registers simultaneously — and knowing which one the moment calls for.

🎨
Design Craft

Raising the quality bar on every surface — systems, interactions, error states, onboarding. Craft is credibility. Without it, strategy is just talk.

🌉
Bridge & Alignment

Connecting product, engineering, support, and leadership around a shared picture of the problem. Not translating between teams — building the language they could all use.

🧭
Leadership & Strategy

Defining what success meant from the customer's perspective — then building the process, the team culture, and the operating model to pursue it consistently.

04 · 4 Moves

From chaos to clarity
in four moves

The sequence mattered as much as the strategy. Each move built the conditions for the next one. You can't systemize what you haven't aligned. You can't instrument what you haven't built. You can't deliver what you haven't measured.

01
Move 1 · Align
Build Clarity

Established structure, ownership, and focus. Grouped 10 roadmaps into 3 priority buckets. Gave teams one language for decisions.

02
Move 2 · Systemize
Create Cadence

Designed an end-to-end framework from intake to handoff. Brought rhythm through sprint planning and design ops. Turned chaos into predictability.

03
Move 3 · Instrument
Guide with Evidence

Built a shared decision pipeline. Mapped review data to UI touchpoints. Shifted the org from tech-driven to user-informed decision-making.

04
Move 4 · Deliver
Deliver System Change

Scaled alignment and trust through Project Janus — a cross-product auth initiative that became a model for future collaboration across the org.

04 · Moves in Detail

Problem, action, result.

Each move followed the same structure: name the real problem, take deliberate action, measure what changed. No move was about features. All four were about changing how the organization worked.

01
Align — Build Clarity
Priority · Process · Language
Problem → Chaos
  • 10 teams, 10 roadmaps — no shared language
  • Endless debates, last-minute scope changes
  • No criteria for what actually mattered
Action → Create Clarity
  • Introduced Priority Query rhythm: decisions vs. exploration
  • Grouped 10 roadmaps into P1 / Debate / Defer buckets
  • Ran Kano study with product + engineering to align on user value
Result → Momentum
  • Teams spoke one language — decisions became predictable
  • Release cadence stabilized into consistent quarterly rhythm
  • Focus shifted from urgency to impact
02
Systemize — Create Cadence
Framework · Rhythm · Ownership
Problem → No Structure
  • No flow from intake → feasibility → design → QA → handoff
  • Meetings without conclusions or owners
  • Last-minute churn from missing feasibility checks
  • Design lacked ownership in the process
Action → Build the System
  • Designed end-to-end framework: Intake → Feasibility → Design → QA → Handoff
  • Structured meeting rhythms: decisions and actions, not discussions
  • Established Jira schema + RACI chart for clarity on roles and gates
  • Taught cross-functional partners to treat design as a strategic function
Result → Trust
  • Reduced rework and last-minute churn significantly
  • Meetings became faster and action-driven
  • Designers spent more time designing, less firefighting
  • Partners saw design as a trusted collaborator — not a bottleneck
03
Instrument — Guide with Evidence
Data · Signal · Authority
Problem → Tech-Driven
  • Product decisions driven by feasibility, not user needs
  • No shared visibility into customer pain points
  • Feedback was noise — unstructured, unprioritized
  • Design decisions lacked credibility without data
Action → Evidence Pipeline
  • Built review tracker: categorized feedback by sentiment + root cause
  • Mapped message logs → UI → support tickets to connect issues with design touchpoints
  • Introduced visibility dashboards so design, support, and engineering shared the same facts
  • Led root-cause workshops: signal vs. noise, first principles
Result → User Focus
  • Teams could see real user pain instead of guessing
  • Auth/login identified as first measurable UX priority
  • Design gained authority in decision-making
  • Org mindset shifted from tech-driven to user-informed
04
Deliver — Project Janus
Authentication · Cross-product · System Change
Insight → "The Front Door Was Broken"
  • Auth flow was #1 user pain: unclear errors, generic CTAs, inconsistent UI
  • Support teams overwhelmed with repetitive, contextless tickets
  • The experience gap eroded trust with every login attempt
  • Fixing login wasn't cosmetic — it was foundational
Action → Craft to System
  • Launched Project Janus: cross-product auth initiative across app, web, and support
  • Redesigned login and error flows — from "Something went wrong" to clear, actionable CTAs
  • Integrated support: personalized replies, SMS confirmations, dedicated help lines
  • Same design principles applied across UI, support scripts, and telemetry
Result → Trust Restored
  • iOS rating: 1.7 → 4.5 ⭐
  • Support tickets: ↓ 72% (1,613 → 451)
  • 422K households impacted, 9.2M sessions stabilized
  • Auth redesign became a model for future cross-product collaboration

07 · Lessons Learned

What this work
actually taught me

Objectives > Operations

Purpose guides process. When teams don't know why they're building something, they optimize for the wrong things. Clarity of objective is the highest-leverage design work.

Design Leadership = Bridging, not Filling Gaps

The job isn't to patch holes. It's to connect people — product, engineering, support — around a shared picture. Gaps fill themselves when alignment exists.

Evidence > Opinion

Data aligns faster than debate. Building the evidence pipeline wasn't a research exercise — it was a political one. Shared facts remove the need for persuasion.

Problem > Solution

The right question matters more than quick answers. Every time we slowed down to define the problem precisely, we shipped something that actually worked.

Clarity & Cadence unlock Creativity

Structure creates space for ideas to thrive. When teams aren't firefighting, they design. When they're not in triage, they think. The system change wasn't administrative — it was creative infrastructure.

Sustainability > Efficiency

Lasting impact over short-term speed. Moving fast on the wrong thing is expensive. The 4 Moves framework was slow to establish and fast to compound.

08 · Future Focus

Freedom & Responsibility

Lead toward independence. The goal is a team that doesn't need me in every room — not because I left, but because I built something that works without me.
