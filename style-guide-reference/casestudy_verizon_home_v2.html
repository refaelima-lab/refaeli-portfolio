01 · Context

I didn't inherit a clean slate.
I inherited a mess with a roadmap.

Verizon Home App was a connectivity management tool for home internet customers — router controls, device management, network diagnostics. On paper, everything a customer would need. In practice, it was a product built to reduce call-center volume, not to serve people.

That one distinction — cost deflection versus customer trust — was embedded in every decision: the information hierarchy, the error states, the onboarding flow. The app was built to make customers stop calling. Not to make them feel capable.

When I joined, there were 1,600+ "app unusable" tickets per release cycle. Teams had tried patching individual screens. Nobody had asked the harder question.

My first three weeks weren't in Figma. I sat with support teams, read customer transcripts, audited 18 months of release notes, and met with every PM and engineering lead I could find.

What I found: eleven teams working in parallel, no shared design language, no defined success metric from the customer's perspective, and an AI strategy that existed in a slide deck but had no design tether.

Most products fail because they solve the wrong thing beautifully. I design systems that resolve what matters, not interfaces that mask it. Good design is invisible. It shapes behavior, until the right thing feels inevitable.

02 · The Problem Beneath the Problem

Three failure patterns.
None of them were about design.

Through research synthesis, customer interviews, and competitive analysis I mapped what was actually breaking. The answers weren't in the UI — they were upstream, in strategy and operating model.

Reactive, not proactive

The app only surfaced when something broke. No ambient presence, no guidance during the calm moments when customers were most receptive to building a relationship with the product.

Error states destroyed trust

When the app failed, it gave customers nothing — no context, no next action, no reassurance. Silence reads as incompetence. Support tickets spiked within 48 hours of every release.

11 teams, no shared language

Every team was solving the same interaction problems in isolation. Without shared components or principles, every sprint added more debt. The experience felt incoherent because it was.

03 · My Role

Principal-level design isn't
the best individual contributor
in the room.

It's changing how decisions get made. Not just what the decisions are. Here's how I framed my mandate — and how that shaped what I prioritized.

01
Redefine the product's reason to exist

Before a single screen, I aligned leadership around a new north star: shift from "reduce calls" to "build trust." This reframed priorities across PM, engineering, and content simultaneously.

02
Design the operating model, not just the product

Established cross-functional rituals — design critiques, shared component libraries, joint sprint reviews — that let 11 teams move with coherence instead of just speed.

03
Give the AI strategy a design tether

Defined what AI-enabled experience actually meant: proactive, contextual, never novelty for its own sake. Established the principle — does this make the right action feel inevitable? If not, it doesn't ship.

04
Build a team that doesn't need me in every room

Mentored by asking better questions, not giving answers. Established critique rituals that raised craft standards without gatekeeping. The goal was a team that could decide without me.

04 · Approach

Sequence matters
as much as craft.

The order in which I attacked problems was as important as how I solved them. Fix trust first. Build infrastructure before features. Earn the room before redefining it.

Months 1–2
Listen before designing

Embedded in support, interviewed engineers, audited 18 months of release notes. Built a shared picture of the problem before proposing anything — this credibility lasted years.

Months 3–4
Fix the trust layer

Redesigned error states and onboarding before building anything new. Trust is cumulative. One bad moment erases ten good ones. We stopped the bleeding first.

Months 5–10
Build the infrastructure

A 900+ component system, cross-platform standards, shared principles. Slowed down initially; by release 4, every team was significantly faster.

Months 11+
Introduce intelligence

With a stable foundation, introduced the AI advisor model — proactive guidance, smart automation. Launched in August 2024. 1M+ customers, 4.5 App Store rating.

01
Home Dashboard

From status board to ambient advisor

The original home screen was a list of settings. It told customers their router was connected. It never told them anything actionable or useful.

The redesign centers around three zones: network health at a glance, an AI insight card surfacing the most relevant action for that customer right now, and quick access to the tasks they return to most often.

Design decision: The AI card gets prime real estate — above the fold, above everything. Because if we're repositioning this product as an advisor, the advice needs to be the first thing you see, not something you have to find.

02
AI Network Advisor

Intelligence that earns its place

The AI strategy existed as a slide deck. It had no design expression — no clear answer to "what does this actually look like, feel like, or say?" My job was to give it one.

The Advisor is not a chatbot. It's a contextual intelligence layer that monitors network health, usage patterns, and device behavior to surface the right insight at the right moment — in plain language, with a clear next action.

Design principle: AI earns trust through restraint. One well-timed insight beats ten generic ones. We established a strict relevance threshold — if an insight doesn't change what a customer would do, it doesn't surface.

03
Self-Healing Error States

Silence was the
real failure

The most impactful design work I did wasn't a new feature. It was redesigning what happens when something goes wrong. The old error state: a spinner, then a dead screen. That single pattern was responsible for hundreds of weekly support tickets.

The redesigned experience treats an error as the beginning of a guided recovery — not the end of a session. Named issues, step-by-step triage, automated diagnostics, and a clear human escalation path when automation isn't enough.

Strategic impact: "App unusable" tickets dropped from 1,600+ to under 460 per release — a 71% reduction. The fix wasn't more engineering. It was designing what recovery actually looked like.

04
Onboarding Redesign

The first 3 minutes
set everything

The original onboarding was a form. Router credentials, account linking, terms. It treated activation as a task to complete, not a relationship to establish.

The redesign opens with what the product will do for you — not what it needs from you. Device detection is automatic. Preferences are set conversationally. By the end, customers have a personalized network without feeling like they filled out paperwork.

Key insight from research: Customers who completed the original onboarding couldn't recall a single feature three days later. First impressions shape memory. We redesigned onboarding to be the product's first act of usefulness — not its first ask.

08 · Reflection

What I'd tell
my earlier self

The most important design work on this project happened before any screen existed. Defining what problem we were actually solving, and making the case for that definition — that's what made everything else possible.

The most important resource I had wasn't craft. It was trust. Trust from PMs to challenge scope. From engineers to push back on timelines. From designers to tell me when I was wrong. That trust was built slowly, through consistency, through being right sometimes and wrong gracefully the rest.

  • Reframe before you redesign. The prettiest solution to the wrong problem is a beautiful failure.
  • Infrastructure is the work. A design system feels like overhead until it doesn't — and then it multiplies everything.
  • Trust is the only metric that compounds. Every withdrawal takes ten deposits to rebuild.
  • Good design is invisible. If customers notice the interface, something went wrong. Success looks like nothing at all.
  • Lead toward independence. The goal is a team that doesn't need you in every room.
